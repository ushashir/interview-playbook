About the Author

Ushahemba Shir

Backend Engineer · Lagos, Nigeria

Ushahemba Shir is a self-taught backend engineer based in Lagos, Nigeria. He builds APIs, microservices, and distributed systems — the kind of software that handles real users, real data, and real failure modes in production. His primary tools are JavaScript, TypeScript, Elixir, and Java, though he has formed opinions about most of the others along the way.

He did not learn through a bootcamp. He does not have a university CS degree. What he has instead is years of building things that broke, debugging things that should not have been possible to debug, and reading source code at hours of the night when documentation ran out. The formal vocabulary came later. The intuition came from the work itself — from encountering the same class of problem in enough different shapes to finally understand what it actually was.

He currently works on production systems where the stakes are real: real latency requirements, real consistency tradeoffs, real security surfaces, real on-call rotations. That grounding shapes everything he writes. When this book explains why an index can hurt you, or when eventual consistency becomes a problem rather than a solution, those are not academic observations. They are things he has watched happen and has had to fix.

He lives and works in Lagos, which means he has built software in an environment where infrastructure is unreliable, bandwidth is constrained, and the engineering solutions that work in a San Francisco data center do not always translate directly. That context has made him a more resourceful engineer and a more honest writer. You will not find advice in this book that only works in ideal conditions.

Why He Writes

There is a problem that shows up everywhere in technical education, and Ushahemba calls it the explanation problem. It works like this: an expert writes an explanation of a concept they mastered years ago. Because they know it deeply, they skip the steps that were actually confusing when they were learning — those steps feel obvious now. The result is an explanation that makes perfect sense to someone who already understands the concept and is useless to everyone else.

Most technical documentation has this problem. Many technical books have it. A huge number of tutorial articles have it. The explanation assumes a level of familiarity that the reader, by definition, does not yet have — because if they had it, they would not need the explanation.

📚 Key Terms

The 5-year-old test: If you cannot explain a concept to someone with no background in the subject and have them follow the core logic, you do not understand it well enough yet. This is not a standard for how you should always explain things — it is a standard for your own understanding. Once you can pass this test, you can explain it at any level of depth the situation requires.

Ushahemba writes the books he wished existed when he was learning. Books that lead with the analogy, not the definition. Books that assume you are smart but do not assume you already know things you came to the book to learn. Books that grow the explanation from simple to sophisticated rather than starting at a level that requires a different book to understand. That teaching philosophy runs through everything he publishes.

His Interview Experience

Ushahemba has been on both sides of the interview table. He has been the candidate who prepared and still failed. He has been the candidate who didn't prepare and somehow passed. And he has been the person sitting across from candidates, evaluating answers, and writing the feedback that candidates never get to read.

That last experience was the most clarifying. When you review candidates, you stop thinking about interviews as tests of knowledge and start seeing them for what they actually are: structured opportunities to observe how someone thinks. The questions are just prompts. What you are listening for is the reasoning beneath the answer — whether someone reaches for tradeoffs instinctively, whether they can hold multiple considerations in their head simultaneously, whether they know where their own knowledge stops and uncertainty begins.

💡 Analogy

Reviewing candidates is like watching two people navigate a city they have never visited. Both might eventually find the right street. The difference is whether they look for landmarks, mentally track their orientation, and ask for help when they are lost — or whether they wander and hope. Interviewers are watching for signs that you are the first kind of person. The answer you arrive at is almost secondary to the route you took to get there.

The things that actually separate senior answers from junior answers in an interview have almost nothing to do with raw knowledge. A junior and a senior candidate often know the same facts. What differs is how those facts are deployed. A senior answer surfaces the tradeoff before being asked. It acknowledges what the proposed solution cannot do, not just what it can. It speaks in terms of consequences — what happens downstream if you make this architectural choice — rather than just features. These are patterns of thinking, not stores of information. And they are learnable. This book is built around teaching them.
