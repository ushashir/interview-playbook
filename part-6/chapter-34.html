Offer, Negotiation, and What Comes After

The company just said "we'd like to extend you an offer." Most candidates say "great, thank you!" and accept within 24 hours. Senior engineers say "I'm very excited — when can I expect the full written offer so I can review it carefully?" Those two responses differ in outcome by about $30,000 a year.

What you'll learn

  • Every component of an offer package and how to evaluate each one honestly
  • How to read equity — options vs. RSUs, liquidation preferences, and what "0.1% of the company" actually means
  • Word-for-word negotiation scripts that are professional, non-awkward, and effective
  • How to handle competing offers and what to do when you don't have one
  • The questions that signal seniority in a final interview stage
  • A 90-day onboarding playbook for starting strong at a new company
Analogy: Buying a House

No reasonable buyer accepts the first asking price. The seller expects negotiation. The agent expects negotiation. The entire process is structured around the assumption that the initial number is the start of a conversation, not a final decision. Job offers work the same way — recruiters extend an initial offer knowing that experienced candidates will respond with a counter. The awkwardness you feel about negotiating is real, and it is costing you money. The awkwardness the recruiter feels is minimal — they've had this conversation hundreds of times. You need to have it once.

1. The Offer Package — What to Actually Evaluate

An offer is not a number. It's a collection of numbers, provisions, and structural conditions that together determine what your next two to four years look like financially and professionally. Evaluate all of them before you respond to any of them.

Base Salary

The most visible number and often the most negotiated. Research market rate before any final-stage conversation — not during it. Use levels.fyi for big tech, LinkedIn Salary, Glassdoor (treat the data directionally, not precisely), and direct conversations with peers in your network. Know your number before they give you theirs.

Equity

Equity deserves its own section (next), but the questions to ask immediately: What type — options or RSUs? What's the vesting schedule? What's the cliff? At startups: what's the current 409A valuation and the strike price? At public companies: what's the current stock price and the grant value?

Signing Bonus

Often more negotiable than base salary because it's a one-time cost for the company rather than a recurring expense. Critically: always ask whether it's guaranteed or performance-based. A guaranteed $20,000 signing bonus is real money. A "performance-based signing bonus of up to $20,000" is a different instrument entirely. Also ask about claw-back provisions — many companies require repayment if you leave within 12 months.

Benefits

Health insurance varies wildly. A plan with low premiums but high deductibles can cost a family thousands per year more than it appears on paper. Ask about: monthly premium cost, deductible, out-of-pocket maximum, and whether dependents are covered. Beyond health: 401k match (and vesting schedule for the match), remote work policy and flexibility, annual learning budget, equipment stipend or company-provided hardware, and parental leave terms.

Title

Title affects your next job negotiation. "Senior Software Engineer" and "Senior Engineer II" at two different companies can describe identical work — but the first title gets you further in interviews at your next company. Ask: does this title accurately reflect the scope of the role? Does the level match what you'd have at a comparable company? Is there room to advance from here?

Team, Manager, and Exit Path

These are not benefits on a PDF, but they may be the most important long-term factors. Have you met the team you'd be joining? Have you had a real conversation with the direct manager — not just an interview? Do you know who's senior on the team and what they're like to work with? And critically: does this role make your next role easier to get? A great title at a company no one has heard of may do less for your career than a more junior title at a company everyone respects. Think about the role as a chapter in your story, not just a compensation event.

Tip: The 48-Hour Rule

Never accept an offer on the phone in real time. "I'm very excited about this opportunity — I'd like to take a couple of days to review the full written offer. Can you send everything over so I can look through it carefully?" This is professional, expected, and gives you time to evaluate without pressure. Any company that pressures you to decide in the same phone call is telling you something about how they operate.

2. How to Evaluate Equity Honestly

Analogy: Lottery Ticket vs. Savings Bond

Startup options are a lottery ticket: potentially life-changing, probably worth zero, depends on factors outside your control. RSUs at a public company are more like a savings bond: you know approximately what they're worth today, the value moves with the stock, and you can sell them after they vest. Neither is automatically better. But they are very different instruments, and you should evaluate them differently.

Stock Options (Common at Startups)

Options give you the right to buy shares at a fixed price — the strike price, also called the exercise price. If the company is worth $10 per share when you join and you have a $10 strike price, your options are "at the money." If the stock price rises to $40 when you vest, you can buy at $10 and immediately have paper gains of $30 per share. But if the company struggles and the stock drops to $8, your options are "underwater" — exercising them means buying at $10 and immediately losing $2 per share. Worthless to exercise.

The question to ask: "What is the current 409A valuation per share?" (The 409A is the IRS-required fair market valuation for private companies.) This tells you the current strike price for new option grants. Then ask: "What was the valuation in the last funding round?" The difference between these two numbers tells you roughly how "in the money" any options would be at the time of grant.

RSUs (Common at Established Companies and Late-Stage Startups)

Restricted Stock Units are grants of actual shares — you don't buy them at any price, you receive them as they vest. At public companies, you know the value: if you receive 1,000 RSUs at a $100 stock price, that's $100,000 in value vesting over four years. At private companies, RSUs are still illiquid — you can't sell them until there's a liquidity event (IPO, acquisition). If neither happens during your tenure, private RSUs may generate a tax bill without any cash to pay it. Ask carefully about the liquidity plan before treating private RSUs as real compensation.

Fully Diluted Share Count

When a startup offers "0.1% of the company," the only way that number means anything is if you know the fully diluted share count. Fully diluted means counting all outstanding shares, all options granted to employees, and all shares reserved in the option pool for future employees. Without this number, 0.1% is meaningless. Always ask: "What is the current fully diluted share count?" Then you can calculate the actual number of shares and what they're worth at the current 409A valuation.

Liquidation Preferences

This is the most important startup equity term that engineers never ask about — and the one that most commonly results in employees receiving nothing in an acquisition that looks successful on paper. Here's how it works: investors who hold preferred stock typically have a liquidation preference, meaning they get paid before common shareholders (employees) in an acquisition. A 1x non-participating preference means investors get their money back first. A 2x participating preference means investors get 2x their money back and then participate in the remaining proceeds alongside common. If the acquisition price is lower than the total preferred investment plus preferences, common shareholders — including you — get zero.

Ask: "What is the liquidation preference structure for the preferred stock?" If the answer is "1x non-participating," that's reasonable. If the answer involves multiples above 1x or participation rights, factor that into your equity valuation. A recruiter who can't answer this question should connect you with the CFO or General Counsel who can.

Warning: Paper Valuation vs. Real Value

"Our last round valued the company at $500M" doesn't tell you what your equity is worth. It tells you what the investors paid for their specific class of preferred shares with specific protections. Your common stock or options may be worth substantially less — or nothing — depending on the preference stack. This is not cynicism. It's how most startup acquisitions actually work.

3. Negotiation Scripts — What to Actually Say

The scripts that follow are not templates to read from. They are patterns to internalize so you can speak naturally in the actual conversation. The goal is to be warm, professional, specific, and unhesitating. The awkwardness of negotiating is entirely in your head. The recruiter has heard these words hundreds of times and is not offended.

On Receiving the Verbal Offer (Never Accept Immediately)

"Thank you so much — I'm genuinely excited about this opportunity.
I'd like to take a couple of days to review the full offer in writing.
Can you send over the written details so I can look through everything properly?
I'll get back to you by [specific day]."

Notice what you are not doing: not accepting, not declining, not giving a number, not apologizing. You are being professional and setting a clear timeline. This is expected behavior.

Making Your Counter

"I've given this serious consideration and I'm very enthusiastic about
the role and the team. Based on my research for this level and location,
and the scope of what you've described, I was hoping we could get to [X].
Is there flexibility there?"

Key rules: give a specific number, not a range. "I was hoping for $145,000" is a negotiation. "I was hoping for something between $140,000 and $150,000" is giving the recruiter permission to offer you $140,000 and call it a win. Give a single number. State a reason (market research, level, scope). Ask a direct question and then stop talking. The silence after "Is there flexibility there?" is theirs to fill, not yours.

If They Can't Move on Base

"I understand the base is constrained. Would there be room to look
at the equity component? Or could we structure a signing bonus that
would bridge the gap in the first year?"

Compensation is multi-dimensional. If one dimension is fixed, move to another. Signing bonuses are often easier to approve than base increases because they're one-time costs. Equity refreshes or accelerated vesting are another lever. Learning budgets, remote equipment stipends, and extra vacation are sometimes negotiable when cash is not.

The Posture Throughout

Warm. Specific. Direct. Not apologetic. You are not asking for a favor. You are providing information about the market and your value, and giving the company an opportunity to match it. You are not threatening to leave. You are not manufacturing urgency. You are having a professional adult conversation about compensation. Companies do not rescind offers over respectful, professional negotiation. If they did, that itself would tell you something important about the culture.

4. Handling Competing Offers

A competing offer is the strongest negotiating lever you have. Use it honestly — never fabricate one.

If You Have a Competing Offer

"I want to be transparent with you: I have another offer I'm considering.
Your opportunity is genuinely my first choice, but I have an obligation
to evaluate both carefully. The other offer is at [company type, not necessarily
name] and is at [$X / a higher base]. I need to make a decision by [date].
Is there anything you can do to make my decision easier?"

You do not have to name the other company. You do not have to show them the competing offer letter. You do need to be accurate about the numbers — don't inflate them. If you say you have an offer at $160,000 and they match it and then find out the real number was $140,000, you have damaged trust before your first day.

If You Don't Have a Competing Offer

Don't manufacture one. Your leverage without a competing offer is market data. "My research shows that the market rate for a senior engineer at this level in this location is $X. I'd like to get there." That's honest and it works. The best time to have leverage in negotiation is when you have alternatives — which is one concrete reason to always be interviewing, even when you're not actively looking to leave.

BATNA — Know Your Walk-Away Number

Key Term: BATNA

BATNA stands for Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement. In a job negotiation, your BATNA is your best option if this negotiation fails — your current job, another offer, freelancing. The strength of your BATNA determines your real leverage. Without a strong BATNA, you negotiate on value. With a strong BATNA, you negotiate on both value and alternatives. Either way, know your walk-away number — the minimum total compensation below which you would decline the offer — before you pick up the phone.

5. Questions That Signal Seniority in Final-Stage Interviews

The questions you ask in a final interview tell the hiring team as much about you as your answers do. Junior candidates ask about salary. Mid-level candidates ask about the team. Senior engineers ask about systems, processes, failure modes, and trajectory. These questions do two things simultaneously: they give you information you genuinely need, and they signal that you think at the level of someone who will be a high-leverage member of the team.

  • "What does success look like in this role after 6 months? After 1 year?" This tells you whether expectations are concrete or vague, whether the role has a real charter or is a grab-bag of tasks, and whether anyone has thought carefully about how to set you up to succeed.
  • "What's the biggest technical challenge the team is facing right now?" This tells you what you'd actually be working on, and whether the technical leadership is honest about problems or only presents the polished version.
  • "How does the team handle disagreements on architectural decisions?" A healthy team has a process — RFCs, ADRs, a forum for technical debate. A team without a process handles it through whoever is loudest or most senior. Know which you're joining.
  • "What does the deploy process look like? How often does the team ship?" A team that ships weekly has a fundamentally different culture than one that ships quarterly. The deploy frequency tells you the health of the CI/CD pipeline, the test coverage, the release process, and the team's relationship with risk.
  • "What does on-call look like? Rotation size, frequency, and typical incident severity?" On-call is the part of the job that affects your life outside work hours. A team of five engineers with a weekly rotation means you're on-call roughly ten weeks a year. Know what you're signing up for.
  • "What would make you hesitate to offer me this role?" This is a bold question that most candidates never ask. It disarms the interviewer, signals confidence and self-awareness, and occasionally surfaces a real concern you can address directly. Used with genuine curiosity rather than defensiveness, it leaves a strong impression.

6. Your First 90 Days

Analogy: Moving to a New City

When you move to a new city, the first 90 days are for learning the streets — where things are, how the traffic moves, which routes work and which are always congested. You don't start suggesting road changes on day three. You learn first, and you earn the right to suggest improvements by demonstrating that you understand why things are the way they are before proposing that they should be different.

Days 1–30: Listen and Observe

Your job in the first month is to understand, not to improve. Read the codebase — not to judge it, but to understand its logic. Read the postmortems, the ADRs, the Confluence pages, the Slack history. Ask "why does this work this way?" before "this should work differently." Set up your environment, understand the deploy process, get familiar with the monitoring and alerting stack.

One specific tactic: find the one or two engineers who have been there longest and ask them to walk you through the history of the system. Every codebase that looks messy has a history that explains why. Understanding that history prevents you from making the same mistakes that were already made.

Days 30–60: Identify High-Leverage Pain Points

By week four or five, you have enough context to start forming opinions. Now is the time to talk with teammates, attend retros actively, and listen for what slows everyone down. The best things to improve in a new role are rarely the flashiest architectural decisions — they're the things that make everyone's daily work slightly worse, compounded across the whole team: a flaky test suite that people have stopped trusting, a deploy process that takes 40 minutes, a monitoring gap that means incidents are always discovered by customers before engineers. Fix one of those and your reputation compounds quickly.

Days 60–90: Ship Something Useful

You need something in production by the end of month two. It doesn't need to be large. It needs to be real, shipped, and useful. This accomplishes several things at once: it builds credibility, it forces you to go through the entire development and deploy process end-to-end, it gives you a concrete conversation piece at your first performance check-in, and it makes you feel like you belong there — which is a surprisingly important mental shift.

The north star: momentum and credibility compound together. A senior engineer who ships something small in week three is taken more seriously in week eight than one who is still "learning the codebase" in week seven.

On the Temptation to Fix Everything Immediately

The most common first-90-day mistake by strong engineers is starting too many improvement conversations before building the credibility to be heard. If you've been there for three weeks and you're already arguing for a full database migration, a new service architecture, and replacing the logging framework — even if you're right about all three — you will be tuned out. Earn the credibility to change important things by being visibly useful on immediate things first.

7. Handling a Low Offer — Specifically

You expected $145,000. They offered $120,000. Here's what you do not do: immediately accept (loses money and signals you were willing to accept less all along) or immediately reject (closes the door before you've negotiated).

What you do: respond within 24–48 hours, warmly, with a specific number and a specific reason.

"Thank you for the offer — I'm genuinely interested in this role
and excited about the team. The base is a bit below what I was expecting
based on my research for this level and location. My research puts the
market around $140,000–$145,000. Is there flexibility in the base,
or could we look at a signing bonus to bridge the gap?"

From that point, prepare for three outcomes:

  1. They meet you. Great. Accept and move forward.
  2. They partially meet you. Evaluate the full package honestly. Is $130,000 base with a $15,000 signing bonus better than $145,000 base? (Yes, in year one. No, starting year two — base affects future raises, bonus calculations, and your starting point at the next company.)
  3. They say no. Now you need your walk-away number. If the offer is below it, you decline professionally. If the offer is above it, you decide whether the non-financial factors are worth the compensation gap.
One More Thing About Low Offers

If a company's initial offer is substantially below market rate, ask yourself why. Sometimes it's a budget constraint that's genuinely fixed — startups have real payroll limits. Sometimes it's a test of whether you'll negotiate (in which case, negotiate). And sometimes it reflects how the company thinks about engineer compensation generally — which tells you something about raises, bonuses, and future offers from the same company. The initial offer is data, not just a number.

Senior vs. Junior: The $25,000 Conversation

The scenario: they offered $120,000. You were expecting $145,000. You have 48 hours to respond.

Junior response: "I guess I'll take it and see how it goes. I don't want to seem ungrateful or risk losing the offer. Maybe I'll ask for a raise after six months." Result: they accept at $120,000. The six-month raise is 3%. They are now at $123,600 — still $21,400 below where they wanted to be, with that gap compounding into every future raise calculation.
Senior response: Waits 24–48 hours — not immediately, because immediate acceptance signals that the first number was fine. Then: "I appreciate the offer and I'm genuinely excited about this role. The base is a bit below what I was expecting based on my research for this level in this location — I was seeing the market around $140,000–$145,000. Is there flexibility to move the base or structure a signing bonus to bridge the gap?" They know their walk-away number before they pick up the phone. They know the three possible outcomes and they've prepared for all of them. They are not apologetic. They are not aggressive. They are professional. The conversation takes seven minutes. The expected outcome, across many such conversations over a career, is tens of thousands of dollars of additional lifetime earnings.

Practice Questions

Beginner

List out the full compensation package from your current or most recent job. Include base, equity (type, vesting schedule, current value), signing bonus, 401k match, health insurance cost, and any other benefits. What's the total annual value? Most engineers have never done this math. It changes how you evaluate future offers.

Intermediate

Role-play this scenario: you've received an offer of $125,000 base. Your research shows market rate is $145,000. Write out the full script of what you would say in the negotiation call — your opening, your counter, your response to "that's the best we can do," and your walk-away line if they don't move. Practice it aloud until it sounds natural and unhesitant.

Advanced

Research a startup offer scenario: a company offers you 10,000 options with a $2.00 strike price, vesting over 4 years with a 1-year cliff. The 409A valuation is $5.00 per share. The last funding round was at $15.00 per share (Series B, $20M raised). The company has issued 50 million fully diluted shares. There is a 1x participating liquidation preference on $15M of preferred stock. Calculate: (a) the current paper value of your fully vested options, (b) what you would receive if the company is acquired for $80M, and (c) what you would receive if the company is acquired for $25M. This is the math that most engineers skip — and it's the math that determines whether startup equity is worth taking.

Summary

  • An offer is a package — evaluate base, equity type and structure, signing bonus terms, benefits, title, team, manager quality, and exit path before responding to any single number.
  • Equity at startups requires specific questions: 409A valuation, fully diluted share count, liquidation preference structure, and liquidity plan — without these numbers, percentage grants are meaningless.
  • Always negotiate: counter with a specific number and a specific reason, ask a direct question, and stop talking — the silence is theirs to fill.
  • Know your walk-away number before any negotiation call; without it you make emotional decisions in real time under pressure that you'll second-guess for months.
  • The interview ends, but the work is just beginning — your first 90 days are your highest-leverage window to build credibility, understand the system honestly, and establish the reputation that will determine how much autonomy and opportunity you're given for the next four years. Ship something by day 30. Listen before you improve. Earn the right to change important things by being visibly useful on immediate ones first. The skills that got you hired are not the skills that will make you exceptional here — those require understanding this specific system, this specific team, and this specific context. Start learning on day one.
