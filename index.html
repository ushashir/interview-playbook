About This Book

Most interview prep books teach you what to say. This one teaches you how to think. There is a difference, and interviewers — both human and AI — can hear it immediately. A candidate who has memorized the definition of a binary tree will give you the textbook answer. A candidate who actually understands it will reach for an analogy first, then confirm you're following, then deepen the explanation based on your reaction. That second candidate gets the job. This book trains you to be that second candidate.

The approach here is deliberate: every concept gets an analogy before it gets a definition. Always. The ELI5 version comes first, then we grow the explanation up to senior-engineer level. This is not dumbing things down — it's the most efficient path to genuine understanding. Once you have the analogy locked in your head, the formal definition snaps into place, and you will never forget it. That's not a teaching trick. That's just how learning actually works.

You will also find real code throughout this book — not pseudocode, not hand-wavy diagrams, not "left as an exercise to the reader." Real TypeScript, real Java, real SQL, real shell commands. When we discuss an algorithm, we implement it. When we discuss an API pattern, we write the route. Code that you can read, run, and understand at a glance is the only kind of code that belongs in a book that's trying to prepare you for a real technical interview.

Finally, this book takes AI-assisted interviews seriously. Platforms like micro1 and HireVue now screen thousands of candidates before a single human ever reads a resume. These systems score you on answer structure, keyword coverage, and communication clarity. Ignoring them is not an option if you are serious about landing a role in 2026. Several chapters address AI screening explicitly, and the preparation advice throughout this book is calibrated to perform well with both human and algorithmic interviewers.

Who Is This Book For?

This book was written with a specific person in mind: a self-taught or bootcamp developer with two to four years of professional experience who is ready to aim for senior roles but keeps running into a wall in the interview process. You can build things. You have shipped to production. You have solved real problems at work. But interviews feel like a completely different game, and you are not wrong — they are.

This book is also for:

  • Career changers who come from non-CS backgrounds and need to quickly fill in the foundational gaps that interviewers probe most often
  • Recent graduates who finished a CS degree but find that academic knowledge and interview performance are two very different things
  • Developers who have failed interviews before — especially those who felt they understood the material but still couldn't perform under pressure
  • Working engineers preparing for their first senior-level search, where the bar shifts and the questions probe depth, tradeoffs, and architectural thinking rather than just syntax recall
💡 Tip

If you have been coding for less than a year, start with Part 2 (Algorithms & Data Structures) and Part 1 (The Human Side) before going anywhere else. Build the foundation. Then come back.

Study Paths by Role

The book is designed to be read cover-to-cover, but most people preparing for interviews have limited time and a specific target role. Use these paths to focus your effort. Parts listed first are highest priority.

Frontend Developer

  • Start: Ch 1–3 (The Human Side) — these chapters apply to every role and every interview
  • Core: Ch 10–15 (Frontend Interviews) — HTML/CSS/JS fundamentals, browser, frameworks, performance, and frontend system design
  • Algorithms: Ch 4–8 (Algorithms) — focus on arrays, strings, trees, and graph traversal; these appear in frontend coding rounds
  • System Design: Ch 29–32 (System Design) — even frontend roles at senior level require architecture thinking

Backend Developer

  • Start: Ch 1–3 (The Human Side)
  • Core: Ch 16–28 (Backend Interviews) — API design, databases, microservices, performance, DevOps, security, and language deep dives
  • Algorithms: Ch 4–8 (Algorithms) — sorting, searching, trees, graphs, and dynamic programming appear regularly in backend coding rounds
  • System Design: Ch 29–32 (System Design) — critical for any backend role above junior level

Full-Stack Developer

  • Start: Ch 1–3 (The Human Side)
  • Algorithms: Ch 4–9 (Algorithms & Data Structures) — the full section; you may be tested on either side
  • Core: Ch 10–15 (Frontend) and Ch 16–22 (Backend core) in parallel — you are covering both worlds, so distribute time proportionally to the role's actual split
  • System Design: Ch 29–32 (System Design) — full-stack senior roles always include an architecture round

Targeting Senior / Lead

  • Start: Ch 1–3 (The Human Side) — senior behavioral questions require a different level of answer; do not skip this
  • Algorithms: Ch 8 (Advanced Algorithms) — the focus here is demonstrating depth and complexity analysis, not memorizing patterns
  • Backend Depth: Ch 19–22 (Microservices, Performance, DevOps, Security) — these are the chapters where senior/junior gaps are most visible
  • System Design: Ch 29–32 (System Design) — this is the primary interview stage for lead and staff roles; spend the most time here
